If the cyborg unsettles boundaries, it does so most forcefully where bodies meet systems. In the present moment, one of the most pervasive sites of this encounter lies in the formation of digital identity as the self today does not reside solely within the physical body nor within the seemingly abstract space of the internet; rather, it emerges across both, assembled through the continuous circulation of data moving between platforms and everyday life. Digital identity therefore cannot be understood simply as an online persona. It is one that manifests simultaneously across digital and material domains, constructed through the replication and distribution of identifiable traits that become legible within all communication.

These traits are often aesthetic, including visual appearance, presentation, lifestyle cues, and modes of self-expression that circulate across algorithmic systems. Through repetition and amplification, such features become recognizable markers of belonging within digital culture, making what appears at first glance to be a personal mode of expression become inseparable from the infrastructures that render it visible and valuable. The digital self is therefore not simply created; it is produced through the design of and participation in networks of platforms and markets. Within this landscape, the promise of digital identity is frequently framed in aspirational terms in its allusive offer of recognition and connection. More subtly, it also promises something more powerful: the prospect of control over one’s own representation and social positioning, framing the ability to construct a desirable digital identity as a form of mastery, implying that individuals can shape their lives simply by carefully curating their appearance, behaviours, and affiliations. At the same time, digital identity also promises belonging by selling the idea that communities may form around shared aesthetic languages, allowing individuals to locate themselves within collective identities that may feel unavailable in physical environments - a promise which is particularly potent in a social context increasingly characterized by fragmentation and isolation, with platforms offering spaces in which identity can be continuously performed and recognized by others who share similar modes of presentation. Yet, the promise extends even further. Control and belonging converge in a third promise: escapism. Digital identity suggests the possibility of transcending the constraints of physical life - social, economic, or bodily - by cultivating a version of the self that appears perfectly optimized, desirable, and oh so free, making this ideal self become both aspiration and instruction.

However, this escape is largely illusory as, in truth, the pursuit of digital identity rarely offers an exit from the systems that shape contemporary life; rather, it frequently intensifies participation within them. With the labour of maintaining an appealing digital self demanding constant attention, consumption, and self-regulation, instead of liberating individuals from systemic pressures, the process of digital self-construction often reinscribes individuals more deeply within the economic and technological structures that produce those pressures in the first place. In this regard, to understand how these promises become so persuasive, it is necessary to look at the systems that sustain them. Among these, fashion occupies a particularly revealing position, as beyond producing clothing, it produces aesthetic value to sell them with, making it possible to define it as a cultural and economic system that transforms bodily presentation into a form of capital functioning just as any other. As Joanne Entwistle argues, fashion markets are fundamentally oriented towards the body and the evaluation of aesthetics (Entwistle, 2009). It is in fact within such markets that aesthetic qualities are not secondary attributes, but the very commodities being exchanged, making participation in these systems therefore required for individuals to embody the aesthetics they seek to circulate or consume, collapsing the distinction between consumption, labour, and identity (Entwistle, 2009). Importantly, this process involves what has been described as aesthetic labour: the work required to produce, maintain, and communicate aesthetic value through bodily presentation (Elias et al., 2017), making it so that practices of self-curation, bodily maintenance, and stylistic adaptation extend far beyond formal employment and become embedded in everyday routines (Widdows, 2018), transforming the body into a site of labour, and shaped through practices designed to align it with prevailing aesthetic standards. Crucially though, these aesthetic systems do not operate in isolation, and instead they are embedded within broader infrastructures of communication that amplify and normalize particular visual languages and symbolisms. With digital platforms accelerating the circulation of images and algorithmic systems rewarding those that conform most effectively to established aesthetic patterns, it is through repetition and visibility that certain aesthetic elements come to function as standards within contemporary visual culture. Still, even though the field here is digital culture and the infrastructure onto which it runs, the mechanisms through which these standards emerge are not entirely new. In fact, historically, aesthetic systems have often operated in close relationship with the same structures of power from which white capitalist patriarchy has given birth to Big Tech. One revealing example can be found in Fascist Italy, where fashion and language were mobilized to construct a coherent national identity. Under Mussolini’s regime, dress was treated not as a superficial matter but as a semiotic system capable of aligning individual bodies with the ideological body of the state (Paulicelli, 2004). For instance, efforts such as Cesare Meano’s Commentario dizionario italiano della moda from 1936 demonstrate how the language of fashion itself was deliberately reshaped in order to stabilize meaning and align aesthetic discourse with the political project of Fascism at its very origin (Paulicelli, 2004). By redefining the terminology of fashion and eliminating foreign linguistic influence, the regime sought to control not only how clothing was described but also how it was culturally understood. This historical case reveals something fundamental: aesthetic discourse can function as a technology of power. By stabilizing meaning and defining acceptable forms of appearance, fashion becomes capable of disciplining bodies and producing compliant subjects, and while contemporary digital culture operates within a different (though not vastly vastly different) political landscape, similar dynamics continue to structure the relationship between aesthetics, communication, and both collective and individual identity.

The disciplining of bodies becomes particularly visible when examined through feminist analyses of beauty culture, with scholars having long demonstrated that aesthetic expectations operate as forms of social regulation, shaping how bodies are perceived and how individuals learn to perceive themselves. Specifically, within digital environments, these dynamics intensify through continuous exposure to idealized images and through technologies that encourage constant self-monitoring (Elias et al., 2017; Wolf, 2002). Practices such as dieting, cosmetic intervention, and aesthetic self-curation can therefore be understood as what Michel Foucault described as technologies of the self: everyday practices through which individuals shape their bodies and behaviours according to socially produced norms (Foucault, 1988; Tynan, 2016); and it is under contemporary conditions, that these technologies increasingly merge with digital infrastructures that encourage users to observe, compare, and optimize themselves in response to circulating images. Surveillance also plays a crucial role in this process, particularly through the expansion of the field of observation beyond traditional institutional models thanks to digital media environments, ultimately producing forms of self-surveillance and peer surveillance that operate simultaneously (Elias et al., 2017), rendering social media platforms optimal ground for individuals to scrutinize their own bodies while also evaluating those of others, creating a distributed system of aesthetic monitoring. In such a systemic dynamic, the body then becomes a site of continuous assessment in which the internalization of the gaze encourages individuals to regulate their own behaviour and presentation in anticipation of being seen and evaluated by others.

It is precisely within this convergence of bodies, technologies, and systems of power that the figure of the cyborg becomes a compass, a metaphor for hybrid existence within technoscientific worlds.

The cyborg refuses clear boundaries between organism and machine, nature and culture, body and technology, revealing the instability of those very same walls.

Viewed through this lens, the contemporary subject of digital culture already inhabits a cyborg condition. Assembled through interactions with technological systems that shape perception, communication, and social participation, bodies become interfaces within networks of technological, economic, social, and aesthetic systems, letting transpire one truth: we are all cyborgs.

-

This project takes up the cyborg in precisely this spirit: as a metaphor. Rather than celebrating technological integration uncritically, it treats the cyborg as a conceptual instrument for examining the entanglements between bodies, aesthetics, and digital infrastructures, enabling it to become a figure that allows us to trace how identities are constructed through hybrid interactions between human practices and technological systems, while also remaining attentive to the power relations embedded within those systems.

The texts that follow therefore approach the cyborg not as a singular object but as a method of inquiry. Through fashion, digital media, and contemporary aesthetic cultures, the project explores how bodies are shaped by technoscientific environments and how those environments, in turn, are shaped by cultural imaginaries of the body. In doing so, the publication assembles a series of perspectives on the cyborg condition of contemporary life, one in which identity is continually produced at the intersection of flesh, image, and machine.
