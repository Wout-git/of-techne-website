If technology is understood broadly as the use of tools that extend the capacities of the body, then fashion emerges as one of humanity’s most enduring technological systems.

Fashion operates through practices that adorn, protect, conceal, and modify the body. Clothing regulates temperature, shields its wearer from environmental conditions, and enables movement across terrains and climates that the body alone could not navigate, while simultaneously functioning as a dense semiotic system through which identity, belonging, hierarchy, and values are communicated. It is then natural to speculate that if tools are technologies, and clothing is produced and enacted through them, then fashion itself can be approached as a technological practice, a system in which human and material capacities intertwine to extend the body into new realms of cultural and material significance.

Even the seemingly simple act of dressing oneself participates in this technological process, as during that very same act the body becomes both medium and instrument in a ritual of continuous exchange with the systems that produce and circulate clothing. From the myriad stages of garment manufacture and those preceding it, to the everyday rituals of wearing, technological integration seeps into the flesh, extending its presence beyond material systems and into social ones. In these exchanges the body emerges as a hybrid configuration of organic and synthetic matter, structured and inscribed with cultural codes, resulting in an enactment of the cyborg through participation in any stage of the fashion system. This hybridity becomes even more tangible when production processes are put into focus. Sourcing materials and producing garments demand intense and extensive collaboration between human skill and technological apparatuses, each tool extending the capacities of the body while also shaping the possibilities of the material itself. Even ostensibly simple tools such as scissors and needles operate as extensions of the body, mediating the translation of imagination into form, and demonstrating how fashion enacts the logic of the cyborg: a system in which human and technological elements converge to generate new material configurations and systems of representation.

Fashion, like the cyborg, exists at the intersection of material engineering, cultural meaning, imagination, and embodied practice, and its significance lies precisely in its ability to merge these domains into hybrid forms. In doing so, fashion turns into a cyborg practice, one in which cyborgs are pieced together to come alive. In fact, clothing does not merely cover or protect the body; it becomes a communicative surface upon which socio-cultural factors are inscribed and performed in order to then be read under that very same lens. In this respect, fashion shares with the cyborg not only a material hybridity but a symbolic dimension, operating as a figure through which societies imagine the relationships between bodies, technologies, and identities, rendering the wearer both subject and instrument, organic and technological.

Recognizing fashion as a cyborg-like system opens a conceptual pathway for understanding the practice of studying fashion itself. Critical fashion research must engage these intersecting dimensions simultaneously, tracing once again the entanglements between material production, cultural meaning, technological mediation, and embodied experience. It is a method that moves beyond surfaces: the visible garment is only the starting point beneath which lie complex networks of labor, infrastructure, ideology, and circulation that constitute its existence and significance. In navigating these layers, critical fashion research mirrors the logic of the cyborg itself: it pieces together heterogeneous elements - historical context, sociological insight, material analysis, technological understanding - into a hybrid form of knowledge capable of generating new understanding of the dressed body and the context in which it becomes such. This practice is also inherently irreverent. Just as the cyborg destabilizes established boundaries between machine and organism, critical fashion research challenges hierarchies that have long dismissed fashion as trivial or superficial. By attending to the intersections of technology, culture, and power within clothing, the researcher disrupts conventional distinctions between the decorative and the analytical, revealing garments as sites where broader social realities, desires, and ideologies converge. In embracing hybridity and interdisciplinarity, critical fashion research enacts a cyborg philosophy: it is insolent, cross-bred, and impure, integrating multiple methods and epistemologies to produce insights that could not arise within any single discipline.

Ultimately, the metaphor of the cyborg illuminates both fashion and its critical study as both foreground entanglement and radical transformation. Fashion demonstrates how bodies have always been technologically mediated through tools, materials, and social codes, while critical research enacts a cyborg logic by navigating surfaces and the networks underneath, linking material, technological, and symbolic dimensions, and generating knowledge that is itself composite and thus transformative. Viewed through this lens, critical fashion research is not merely a methodology or academic discipline, it is a cyborg practice in itself, an interdisciplinary way of thinking that illuminates how bodies, technologies, and cultural meanings converge to shape the lived realities of everyday life, past, present, and future.
